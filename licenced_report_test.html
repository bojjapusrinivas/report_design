COVID-19-Led Disruptions Exhaust Radiopharmaceuticals Supply to Hinder Nuclear Medicine Procedures in Most Countries

The COVID-19 pandemic has disrupted supply chains and left various countries struggling with availability of radiopharmaceuticals used in molecular imaging, with low-income nations feeling the heat the most. The crisis has resulted in supply issues associated with radiopharmaceuticals, radioisotopes and kits for therapeutic and diagnostic applications. While the US accounts for nearly half of the market, it relies on three suppliers of Molybdenum-99 and Technetium-99m. In addition, majority of African nations depend heavily on one supplier of these radioisotopes that are used for cancer imaging. The COVID-19 outbreak has complicated the scenario owing to ongoing restrictions on flights that have severely disrupted global supply chains, prompting participants to ensure reliable radiopharmaceuticals supply for nuclear medicine. Participants are also facing issues in procuring cold kits for simplifying radiopharmaceutical production. These supply constraints along with shortage of qualified staff has made it challenging for countries to perform tasks such as labeling and quality assurance, hampering complex procedures. There are issues related to particular radiopharmaceuticals. While 18F-FDG is extensively used, non-FDG tracers remained in limited quantities across low-income nations owing to their extensive nature, regulatory restrictions and lack of cyclotron. In addition, various countries are unable to access therapeutic tracers including 123I, 123I-MIBG, and 131I-MIBG due to supply and cost issues. The high relevance of radiopharmaceuticals in the field of nuclear medicine led countries and leading organizations to cooperate for ensuring proper supply of these products to facilitate delivery of quality care to patients.

However, the scenario remains challenging for non-COVID-19 treatment solutions such as medical radioisotopes, which are used in diagnosis and treatment of cancer and certain health conditions by healthcare providers around the world. Every year, millions of patients are administered with radioisotopes for diagnosis and therapeutic purposes. With governments defining the medical radioisotope production facilities as ‘essential’, production operations continued during the COVID-19 pandemic period. Reactors engaged in production of radioisotopes remained operational in compliance with the relevant IAEA safety standards and in adherence to relevance measures to prevent the effects and spread of COVID-19 disease. Reactors based out of Argentina, Australia, Belgium, Czech Republic, Netherlands, Poland, Russia , USA, and South Africa, account for almost the entire world production of radioisotopes, and the radio compounds produced in these countries are transported worldwide mainly via aerial route. Due to COVID-19 containment measures and border closures, several airlines have temporarily halted their operations, while affecting the distribution of radioisotopes. Because of the supply chain issues, several hospitals have delayed diagnosis applications and have been forced to reschedule interventions. Also, most research and education programs involving isotopes have been put on hold. Because of challenges in the cross-border distribution and flight cancellations, and subsequent low demand for radioisotopes produced, most reactors are running their units below the normal levels. The challenging scenario is forcing the reactors to scale down their operations and even opt for temporary shutdown. Although some countries and enterprises are running charter flights for the delivery of medical supplies including radiopharmaceuticals, the overall supply chain scenario currently remains challenging.

The imposition of national lockdown in South Africa, a major producer of medical radioisotopes, to control the spread of the virus has affected supply. Despite these restrictions, the Safari-1 research reactor along with Nuclear Energy Corporation of South Africa, a division of NTP Radioisotopes, continued production of medical radioisotopes to ensure supply. However, limited production as a result of concerns associated with trans-border distribution coupled with cancellation of certain production batches impeded supply. While these producers are making serious efforts to boost output and resume distribution to countries, especially countries hit hard by COVID-19, supply volumes are anticipated to be considerably less than the required demand.

Radiopharmaceuticals: A Prelude

Radiopharmaceuticals are medicinal solutions containing radioisotopes or radiotracers, which are administered to the patient where they localize to specific organs and emit radiation. Radiopharmaceuticals generally comprise a radioactive isotope and a targeting entity such as a peptide or an antibody. These radiopharmaceuticals can be administered to patients orally, or through, parenteral route or IV injections or placed in the body cavity. Radiopharmaceuticals are used for both diagnostic and therapeutic applications.

A radiopharmaceutical, also known as a radionuclide or a radioactive tracer, is sterile, pyrogen-free, and safe for in vivo administration in humans for the purposes of diagnosis or therapy. Radiopharmaceuticals cause no pharmacological effect as only a small volume of the material is administered. A radiopharmaceutical is composed of two parts, the radioisotope and the pharmaceutical, which are attached together through a process called labeling. A cyclotron situated at the hospital manufactures the radioisotope. When the radioisotope is prepared, it is sent to a laboratory where it is conjoined to a pharmaceutical, thus creating the radiopharmaceutical.

Radiopharmaceuticals detect and characterize disease states, or normal biological function, in humans, living cells or animals. When used as tracer molecules, radiopharmaceuticals can map the distribution, uptake as well as metabolism of the molecule in basic research, applied research or clinical studies. Diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals provide detailed description of the anatomy and functioning of internal body organs.

Certain radiopharmaceuticals consist of radionuclides that deliver particulate emission such as beta or alpha radiations or auger electrons possessing the capability to kill diseased tissues. Therapeutic radiopharmaceuticals act as palliative or curative agents for treating critical diseases, such as cancer and tumors.

When used as tracer molecules, radioactive isotopes facilitate elaborate medicinal and biological studies. Commonly used radionuclides for research purposes include 31H and 146 C. Radiation can be effectively used for sterilizing surgical instruments in hospitals as well as final packaged containers of pharmaceuticals. Antibiotics, hormones, vitamins and other thermolabile materials can be safely sterilized. 

Select commercially available Radiopharmaceuticals include Xofigo (Radium-223), Metastron (Strontium-89), Amyvid (Fluorine-18), Quadramet (samarium 153), ProstaScint (Indium-111), Cardiogen-82 (Rubidium-82), Glofil-125 (Iodine-125), Zevalin (Yttrium-90), Ceretec (Technetium-99m), among others. 
Mechanism of Action

Radiopharmaceuticals are combinations of a radionuclide, linked together with a carrier molecule (an antibody or peptide). Radionuclide (a radioactive element such as uranium or iodine) relies upon different isotopes of the same element (such as iodine-123), which is used for imaging purposes because it produces gamma radiation with high penetrating power. The radiation decay or emissions will determine the type of application for which the radiopharmaceutical can be used. The carrier molecule is chosen based on the metabolic characteristics of a specific disease, which has the capability to determine where in the body the radioisotope will gather. A radiopharmaceutical is introduced into the body by way of injection, inhalation or swallowing. The radioisotopes present in radiopharmaceuticals spontaneously generate specific amounts of radiation, which could be used to diagnose and treat a range of human injuries and diseases. As a result, radiopharmaceuticals are capable of mapping physiological function as well as metabolic activity in a non-invasive manner unlike other imaging modalities like X-rays, MRI that can only point out the anatomical differences of the target organ.
Global Supply Shortages Continue to Challenge Market Growth

A diminishing worldwide supply of Tc-99m that is used to radiolabel over 80% of the radiopharmaceuticals continues to pose a major challenge to the global radiopharmaceuticals market. Worldwide requirement of 99Mo is estimated at 12000 Curie (Ci) per week ‘6 day’ calibrated. Technetium-99 is the most common medical radioisotope, used in around 80% of the nuclear medicine procedures translating to approximately 30-40 million medical diagnostic procedures annually. The parent isotope of Technetium-99, Mo-99 is produced by fission of highly enriched uranium (HEU) targets across aging research and material testing nuclear reactors in several countries located in Europe, Canada, South Africa, and Australia. Separation of Mo-99 from irradiated uranium targets is performed at processor facilities. Purified Mo-99 is shipped to generators and subsequently to vendors/radiopharmacies where Tc-99m is mixed with pharmaceuticals. There is no Mo-99 production currently in the US, the largest market in terms of demand. Supply stability of Mo-99 and related infrastructure is of critical importance to patients that rely on nuclear medicine. Globally, there are about 50 research reactors producing radioisotopes. However, only six major reactors exist that produce large amounts of Molybdenum-99. These include NRU in Canada, HFR in the Netherlands, OSIRIS in France, BR2 in Belgium, and SAFARI in South Africa, and OPAL in Australia. Majority of the reactor produced Molybdenum-99 is finished at one of five finishing sites, namely Nordion (Canada) Inc., Mallinckrodt in the Netherlands, NTP Radioisotopes (Pty) Ltd. in South Africa, Institute for Radioelements in Belgium, and ANSTO in Australia.

Supply reliability of TC-99m has become a cause of concern over the past decade, primarily due to unplanned or extended shutdowns at some of the ageing research reactors and processing facilities worldwide. At present global supply of Mo-99 remains challenged with most of the reactors being well past their prime since they became operational over 4-5 decades ago. These reactors have also come under intense scrutiny due to frequent maintenance issues, hence impacting the global Mo-99 supply chain. Some of the scheduled shutdowns are a result of ageing of reactors producing Mo-99 and the longer maintenance periods incurred. Since the radioisotopes, Mo-99 and Tc-99m have short half-lives of 66 hours and six hours respectively, they are difficult to stockpile. As Mo-99 is required to be delivered to imaging centers weekly, any disruption in its supply results in impacting critical diagnostic medical imaging tests throughout the world. Given the rising demand for Mo-99 in nuclear medicine procedures, supply shortages, if left unaddressed, would significantly affect the usage and applications of Tc-99m. Security implication of highly enriched uranium is another factor restraining the supply chain. Over the recent years, several initiatives to reduce usage of HEU have been undertaken to reduce nuclear terrorism threat. Except OSIRIS and BR2, other global reactors have converted to LEU fuel. However, they still irradiate HEU targets, given that their processors do not plan to convert to LEU from HEU, or are in the shift process.

It is expected that supply of radioisotopes is poised to remain tight in the near future because of closure of major reactors in the Netherlands and Canada, impeding the global radiopharmaceutical market. It is difficult to transport the short half-life radioisotopes to other regions where the raw material production is negligible, as these compounds degrade rapidly. Supply of radioisotopes will deflate at a moderate pace because of  processing limitations such as communications, transport, and demand management, thus limiting the growth of radiopharmaceutical market to a certain extent.

Outlook

Growth in the radiopharmaceuticals market is supported by growing number of nuclear procedures driven by rise in prevalence of cancer, and heart diseases. Nuclear medicine is commonly employed in cardiac/brain/spleen/kidney imaging, detection of gastrointestinal issues, and bone scans for cancer diagnosis. Increasing number of these procedures continue to drive demand for radiopharmaceuticals. The market is further supported by the recent discovery of newer and successful therapeutic applications of radiopharmaceuticals in oncology and cardiology prognosis. Significant therapeutic usage of radiopharmaceuticals became possible after the availability of cyclotrons and nuclear reactors which could synthesize large quantities of radioisotopes. Also driving growth in the market is rise in incidence of neurological disorders. Continuous breakthrough, particularly in the fields of research and clinical applications are further expected to assist sustained growth in the radiopharmaceuticals market.

Additional impetus from a stream of drug development and testing phases demonstrate the interest surrounding radiopharmaceuticals. Development and progress in this space is anticipated to be fueled by immunology combined with radioisotopes and monoclonal antibodies so as to produce radioisotopes with unprecedented specificity in cancer diagnosis and therapy. Rising popularity of advanced SPECT/CT and PET/CT scanners is also expected to unravel new clinical opportunities. Rise in PET procedural volume is expected to provide opportunities for radiopharmaceuticals such as Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG). Additionally, the use of PET-CT complemented by SPECT-CT would further improve growth prospects of the diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals in the coming years.

Despite steady growth prospects, the radiopharmaceutical market is confronted with challenges, which include volatile supply chain of Tc-99, the most common diagnostic radioisotope used in around 30 million procedures annually. At present, the anticipated closure of aging nuclear reactors and logistical difficulties associated with their imports pose an important challenge before companies to find alternatives to address the growing medical imaging requirements. Other hindrances to growth include unfounded fear over radiation exposure, high cost of equipment requiring the use of radioisotopes, short half-life of radioisotopes, dearth of lack of good manufacturing practices, limitations on reimbursement, and stern regulatory approvals However, growing interest in PET imaging in drug-development as well as expanding applications of imaging modalities into the fields of cardiology and neurodegenerative conditions owing to the introduction of novel and advanced radiotracers is expected to sustain market growth. However, the key factor that is expected to drive the radiopharmaceuticals market’s future growth is the introduction of next-generation therapeutic nuclear medicines.
Key Market Drivers

Growing Acceptance in Cancer Treatment

Application of radiopharmaceuticals in the cancer diagnosis and therapy goes back to more than 5 decades. The fast increasing global population along with growing prevalence of cancer ensures strong growth opportunities for radiopharmaceuticals. Growth is primarily attributed to the increase in use of scanning as well as imaging techniques for tumor detection and higher rate of adoption of nuclear medicine processes. Increasing disposable incomes in emerging economies have also resulted in improved standard of living, thereby ensuring use of improved medical technology in detection and treatment of chronic diseases such as cancer.

Increasing Acceptance of Disease-Targeted Treatment

Usage of radiopharmaceuticals in targeted treatment is gaining acceptance due to widespread research at both preclinical and clinical stages, conducted on a global scale. Easy detection of metastatic sites and uninterrupted monitoring of progress in treatment emerged as key drivers of market growth of radiopharmaceuticals in targeted treatment applications. Such applications also facilitate higher dosage delivery of medications and shorter stay in hospital, consequently making nuclear treatments more cost efficient than other cancer therapies. Patients going through routine diagnostic processes for instance, MRI and ultrasound scans need several numbers of scans to reach a specific diagnosis, whilst nuclear imaging facilitates such procedures with a single scan.

Growing Emphasis of Accurate Diagnosis

Increasing prevalence of cancer as well as cardiovascular diseases across the world emphasizes the requirement for effective diagnostic procedures which is extremely precise and cost-efficient. Molecular imaging processes utilizing radioisotopes are becoming popular primarily because of simple administration and ultra-precision. More than 100,000 diagnostic procedures are conducted across the globe using radiopharmaceuticals.

Expanding Molecular Imaging Applications

Cardiovascular and oncology imaging represent the core applications and growth drivers for diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals market, given the increasing incidence of heart disease, stroke, and cancer among aging population. However, continual research on radiopharmaceuticals has expanded its use in other applications such as hyperthyroidism therapy, infection imaging and neurology. Innovative isotopes are under study for use in nuclear imaging and therapeutic applications. Also, diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals are being increasingly used in diagnosing cancer and neurological diseases, such as Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease, which tend to be widespread among the aging population. Radiopharmaceuticals flaunt the potential to offer better disease management to the elderly suffering from cognitive conditions by providing early disease identification, reduced cost of healthcare and improved quality of life.
Market Restraints

Market-challenging factors include high investment needed for infrastructure, stringent regulations and requisite FDA approvals, high cost of nuclear medicinal imaging procedures, and the short half-life of isotopes resulting in global supply shortage. Closure of major reactor plants in the immediate future is also expected to significantly affect the market. Other market-restraining factors include reimbursement/pricing issues, lack of dosage standards for pediatric administration, radiation exposure fears, and the underpenetrated markets of developing countries.

Stringent Regulatory Guidelines for Manufacture, Storage and Usage

Radioisotopes are unpopular on account of the stigma associated with radiation. Fear related to radioactivity among patients and hospital bodies is projected to impact the uptake of radiopharmaceuticals. Consequently, the guidelines of regulatory bodies for manufacture, transportation, and storage of such molecules is extremely strict, involving stringent production guidelines, strict regulatory procedures to gain FDA approval, supply chain effectiveness, and safety handling procedures. Besides, the fears relating to radiopharmaceutical exposure and radiation overdose are significant factors which are responsible for limiting growth in the market. In addition, concerns relating to safety of radioisotope manufacturing process involve the utilization of HEU (highly enriched uranium) for the Mo-99 manufacture. The weapons-grade uranium, HEU, is denounced by government across the world because of potential risks of nuclear terrorism. On the other hand, utilization of LEU (low grade enriched uranium) is being promoted.

Price Sensitivity & Competition from Alternatives

Reimbursement remains a key factor influencing growth in the radiopharmaceutical market. Market acceptance of products is heavily dependent on the ability of consumers to obtain adequate reimbursement from third-party payers. Government programs including Medicaid and Medicare, and private healthcare insurance has limited the coverage and amount of reimbursement for a particular procedure or treatment. Such policies have created, and aggravated price sensitivity among consumers, thus thwarting the market potential of key radiopharmaceuticals. In addition, in a managed care environment, outcomes and cost savings are touted to be the prime variables in encouraging product acceptance and determining its commercial success. High cost sensitivity has been the stumbling block in expansion of nuclear medicine procedures. Utilization of nuclear diagnostics is also restricted due to limited physician and patient base and availability of relatively cheaper imaging modalities such as X-ray, MRI and CT.

Lack of Dosage Standards for Pediatric Patients Limits Radiopharmaceutical Efficacy

Although there exist well-entrenched guidelines for administering radiopharmaceutical dosages for adults, majority of radiopharmaceutical package inserts fail to offer guidance on the diagnosis and treatment of pediatric disorders. While imaging children, clinicians usually base dosages on the type and nature of disease, patient's body weight, the availability of equipment and professional experience. Radiopharmaceuticals are administered differently in young children and newborns, as circulation time in infants is faster than in adult patients. Differences in kinetic and metabolic activities, body sizes, wide range of disorders, and disease stages necessitate customized approaches to pediatric molecular imaging. Low doses do not allow adequate examination, and conversely, high doses do not yield enhanced diagnostic accuracy suggesting an intrinsic need to standardize the minimal amount of pediatric radiopharmaceutical doses for satisfactory examinations.

Unfounded Fears of Radiation Exposure Hinder Growth

Whilst there is much exhilaration about the potential benefits offered, myths and misconceptions continue to surround radiopharmaceuticals. For instance, unfounded fears over excessive radiation exposure among patients continue to frustrate patient compliance. Nuclear diagnostics utilizes only a small volume of radioactive compounds, thus patients risk from the test radiation is comparable to, or sometimes lower than that of other medical procedures, such as x-rays. However, consumer skepticism about the technology from lack of knowledge could repress its development and use.

Bottlenecks in Developing Markets

Availability of radioisotopes is crucial for the widespread adoption of diagnostic and therapeutic radiopharmaceuticals in diagnosing and treating chronic diseases. Unlike developed nations, factors such as inadequate local production, and logistical difficulties associated with product imports limit the market potential for radiopharmaceuticals in developing nations. Scarcity of key resources such as capital investment and qualified personnel severely inhibits the application of this technology. Lack of awareness among practicing physicians, accessibility to equipment and infrastructure additionally impede growth. The need of the hour is therefore for setting up indigenous production facilities and commitment of resources, which will help boost market prospects for radiopharmaceuticals in developing nations.
Recognition of Intrinsic Cost Benefits: Critical to Market Acceptance

Although nuclear imaging is costlier compared to other imaging modalities, the accuracy and precision provided far outweigh the costs involved. The ability of nuclear medicine procedures to enable early disease detection and initiate prompt treatment regimens help cut down healthcare expenses and the number of procedures required. In addition, they also offer better patient outcomes, disease management, and quality of life. Nuclear medicine utilizes diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals that are capable of detecting minute abnormalities and providing sensitive images with superior clarity, thus playing a pivotal role in the cost effectiveness of preventive medicine. As managed healthcare continues to evolve, efforts of radiopharmaceutical manufacturers are expected to be directed towards development of new treatment indications for new diseases where alternative modalities lack a strong presence.
Classification of Radiopharmaceuticals

Based on the use of radiopharmaceuticals for either diagnostic or therapeutic purposes, the market can be classified into Diagnostic Radiopharmaceuticals, and Therapeutic Radiopharmaceuticals. Although diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals continue to remain core to the nuclear pharmacy industry, there has been rising interest in the emergence and use of therapeutic agents during the last decade. The shift in focus has been profound primarily due to research involving combination of radioactive isotopes with advanced molecular carriers.
Diagnostic Radiopharmaceuticals: The Dominant Segment

Radiopharmaceuticals used solely for the purpose of medical diagnosis are called diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals. Intended for use in diagnosing or monitoring of diseases in humans, diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals comprise of radiolabeled isotopes linked to a carrier molecule that can be utilized for imaging purposes to view or diagnose specific conditions and diseases. Administered internally to the patient prior to the imaging procedure, the radionuclide component gives out a small dose of radiation, and thus enables scintigraphic imaging. Inside the body, the carrier molecule travels to the target area or organ enabling the radiolabeled isotope to accumulate at the site. The accumulated radioactive particles emit gamma rays to image the particular site of interest, utilizing a gamma camera. Radiopharmaceuticals are extremely time sensitive, and are subject to quick radioactive decays that lead to radical changes in inherent composition. Radiopharmaceuticals are therefore semi-manufactured and come in the form of radionuclide generators, precursors and kits, and the final preparation is made immediately before administration to the patient.

Diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals not only offer sensitive images with high visual clarity, but also enable physicians to visualize changes and behavior of disease in real time. These agents provide images of specific physiological processes. Diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals represent a cost effective alternative to other imaging modalities by detecting very minute abnormalities that cannot be detected by other modalities. A single injection of diagnostic radiopharmaceutical can be used for imaging small cell lung cancer instead of conventional five stage steps. This results in significant healthcare savings by avoiding unnecessary diagnostic procedures. Diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals are additionally non-invasive in nature and are invaluable in investigations that require a detailed examination of the level of blood flow to the brain, liver functioning, lungs, heart and kidney function, and identifying the extent of bone damage. These high priced agents comprise a radionuclide, and a non-radioactive component. Technetium-99m, iodine-123, indium-111, fluorine-18, gallium-66, 67, 68, and copper-64 represent examples of radionuclides.

Cardiology diagnostics include thallium and technetium based products used principally for myocardial perfusion studies conducted by means of gamma cameras.

Non-cardiology diagnostics include all radioactive diagnostic agents that are not cardiac imaging agents, and utilized for diagnostic imaging by means of gamma cameras.

Positron Emission Tomography (PET)

PET diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals are employed in procedures utilizing PET/CT or PET diagnostic scanners while SPECT diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals are employed in procedures utilizing gamma cameras.

Positron Emission Tomography (PET) allows for detailed study of an organ’s metabolic activity unlike anatomic imaging modalities such as computer tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), which provide for only structural investigation. In other words, PET is a sophisticated molecular imaging technique, which brings together computer-aided technology with chemical procedures to produce 3D colored images of organs or substances functioning inside the body. This technique requires short-lived radioisotopes that are generated in a cyclotron and appended to a biochemical or sugar molecule, along with an advanced technology scanner. Cyclotron radioisotopes are essentially positron emitting radionuclides, which when injected into the patient accumulate in the target tissue. The chemical then emits a positron that binds with an electron resulting in simultaneous emission of two identifiable gamma rays, which are traced and picked up by a camera. The gamma rays are recorded as a signal by photomultiplier-scintillator detectors located close to the body. Diagnostic agents for PET examine the initial biochemical changes that transpire as a precursor to disease. The biochemical changes, such as high metabolic activity associated to tumor growth, make PET a suitable tool for early diagnosis and treatment.

A prime reason responsible supporting growth in PET is approval of newer indications in oncology and use in breast cancer, melanoma, lung cancer, colon cancer and lymphoma.

Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT)

SPECT is the commonly used form of tomographic imaging that utilizes a gamma camera to capture multiple images from multiple angles. Usually, SPECT cameras are employed with radiopharmaceuticals having longer half-lives in comparison to radiopharmaceuticals used with PET. SPECT emit high-energy photons called as gamma rays rather than positrons. SPECT radiopharmaceuticals include peptides and antibodies, which can be targeted to specific tissue receptors, facilitating clinicians to distinguish diseased and healthy tissue. Integration of SPECT with CT allows physicians to cautiously analyze the location and size of radiopharmaceutical uptake, thus improving diagnostic accuracy. As SPECT is primarily based on radiopharmaceuticals such as tecnetium, logistics of handling SPECT agents is comparatively lower than PET. While PET-CT is significant in oncology, potential applications of SPECT-CT include cardiology, general nuclear medicine, neurology, and oncology to name a few.

Therapeutic Radiopharmaceuticals

Therapeutic radiopharmaceuticals are radiolabeled isotopes linked to a chelating agent or a carrier molecule, to allow therapeutic radiation for treating specific conditions or disease states. Inside the body, a therapeutic radiopharmaceutical emanates particulate radiation that can only travel short distances in body tissues, to either damage or destroy the surrounding cells of the target organ or the area to which it has been distributed. Therapeutic radioisotopes are different from diagnostics radioisotopes principally based on the kind of radiation produced by the radioisotope.

Targeted radionuclide treatment encompasses the employment of radiopharmaceuticals to carefully supply cytotoxic radiation levels to a disease location, to potentially supply the absorbed radiation dosage more carefully to cancerous tissues. Advancements in solid phase peptide synthesis, tumour biology, radiopharmaceuticals chemistry and recombinant antibody technology, result in development of multiple innovative radiotherapeutic agents.

Radio-labelled peptides in the form of molecular vectors are being created for targeted treatment. Once they are labeled with radionuclides (therapeutic), peptide molecules is available with the capability of destroying receptor-expressing tumors, a method known as PRRT (peptide receptor radionuclide therapy). Yttrium-90 (Y-90) and Lu-177 are often utilized as radionuclides in these PRRT studies. The evaluation of the relative efficiency of various radiopharmaceuticals for cancer treatment is complex through the development, due to the huge quantity of variables to be measured, a few associated with the biological carrier and the remaining with the radioisotope.

Emergence of Radiotheranostics: Dual-Functioning Radiopharmaceuticals

Theranostics (also called theragnostics) is a relatively new and expanding field of medicine that refers to procedures combining diagnosis and treatment used to target specific tumors and diseases. In nuclear medicine, theranostics refers to the use of diagnostic radionuclides such as positron or gamma emitters with therapeutic radionuclides such as beta or alpha emitters, for diagnosis and therapy of a particular malignancy. Growing interest in theranostics is being driven by advances radioisotope-based therapeutics (radiotheranostics); bioimage-guided radiotherapy delivery; optotheranostics; interventional oncology; nanotherapeutics; and basic sciences. Advances in theranostics, including the development of new diagnostic and therapeutic agents for use in the identification, diagnosis and treatment of neuroendocrine tumors will enable personalized treatment based on the very specific type of cancer and the specific tumor cell membrane proteins that each patient may have. The development of modalities such as peptide receptor scintigraphy (PRS) and peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT) through the process of radiolabeling of somatostatin analogs with various radionuclides widened the scope of theranostics for oncology indications.

Radiotheranostics is a clinically advanced application of theranostics, wherein imaging and therapeutic intervention are closely linked. Radiotheranostics uses radiolabelled drugs that simultaneously target and permit detection by medical imaging such as PET-CT or SPECT-CT to irradiate cancer cells and eliminate them. The notable examples of radiotheranostics are those agents that target somatostatin receptors (SSTRs) in neuroendocrine tumors, prostate-specific membrane antigen in prostate cancer, and chemokine receptors in multiple myeloma. Radiotheranostics is anticipated to play a major role in the management and care of patients with cancer. Furthermore, the development of new radioisotopes is expected to improve the therapeutic efficacy of radiotheranostics in cancer care. For example, Lutetium-177 dotatate received US FDA approval for treatment of neuroendocrine tumours. Lutetium-177 dotatate is currently being explored for the treatment of pheochromocytoma, paraganglioma and meningioma. Radiotheranostics are also being explored for non-cancer applications such as severe arthritis.

PET Eclipses SPECT in Cardiovascular Imaging

PET (positron emission tomography) is regarded to be eclipsing SPECT for application in the field of cardiac imaging. Even as PET scans are being regarded superior, technological advances are expected to resuscitate the market for SPECT systems. While the introduction of novel radiotracers is expected to boost the market for PET imaging, advances in technology is likely to promote improvement of image quality in SPECT systems. Innovative iterative reconstruction software, such as the UltaSPECT, is intended to improve the quality of images in SPECT primarily by boosting the signal-to-noise ratio.

Traditionally SPECT/CT has been primarily used in cardiac imaging, while PET/CT found increased application in oncology imaging. However shortage of Tc-99m which is largely used in SPECT/CT for cardiac imaging resulted in migration towards PET myocardial perfusion imaging (MPI). While PET exhibits higher sensitivity and specificity, improvement in Tc-99m supply in near future is expected to drive the uptake of SPECT.

Resolution of images is touted to be vital to the success of PET/SPECT imaging devices. PET is known to offer a resolution of up to 7 mm in cardiac scanning; while cardiac resolution in SPECT systems is known to extend up to 15 mm. However, studies indicate that PET offers superior diagnosis owing to its improved hardware components which enable the scan to identify the presence of microvascular triple-vessel disease, often undetected by a cardiac SPECT system. PET’s superior diagnostic ability is attributed to advances such as quantification, leveraging numerical precision to assist in identifying of defects in the heart, generally undetected by a qualitative SPECT. Yet another difference is the half-life of the isotope used in each of the modalities. Radioisotopes such as technetium-99m offer a half-life of about 6 hours, while radioisotopes such as rubidium-82 used in PET is deemed to have a half-life of about 75 seconds, leaving no margin for error in cardiac PET scans while restricting the ability to implement stress testing. With both imaging modalities enjoying advantages as well as disadvantages over the other, the debate over PET and SPECT continues to dwell in the industry.

Cardiac Amyloidosis Visualization: A Potential Application for PET

Cardiac amyloidosis, which is characterized by abnormal protein deposits in the heart, is a disease that is very difficult to diagnose. Abnormal protein deposits or amyloidosis is known to eventually lead to affected organ dysfunction, while the prognosis is worse when heart becomes the affected organ. Symptoms and signs of the condition are non-specific, and there is no definitive non-invasive test that can accurately diagnose cardiac amyloidosis. Biopsy of heart muscle is the only way to diagnose the condition. However, positron emission tomography was recently discovered to have the capability to diagnose this condition with the use of radiotracer F-18 –florbetaben, as per researchers at the Princess Alexandra Hospital, in Brisbane, Australia. Researchers used patients with hypertensive heart disease-led thickened myocardium as controls for comparison against thickening that follows amyloid deposition. The visualization and quantification of the deposition through PET offers a non-invasive and accurate diagnostic means for the disorder. The research established that PET imaging using F-18-florbetaben can differentiate between hypertensive heart disease and cardiac amyloidosis, accurately. The diagnostic technology could further be investigated for use in other factors, non-hypertensive and non-amyloid causes, leading to heart muscle thickening.

Personalized Medicine Drives Nuclear Medicine

The traditional healthcare setting is more of a trial-and-error method of treatment, subjecting both patients and physicians to a great degree of uncertainty. In case of patients, the uncertainty entails greater cost, both physically and monetarily. This is evident from several drug withdrawals in the market, such as Rezulin, Fen-phen and Baycol, which have shown serious side effects on patients.

These uncertainties have led to redefining the way drugs are developed. Resultantly, the role of customized treatment has become more important in the healthcare scenario over the past few years. Analyzing and understanding the genetics behind a patient’s response to a certain therapy would allow the physician to make a safer and effective medical decision. In addition, the genetic analysis of the patient would allow the physician to identify the predisposed disease risk in the patient and prevent the disease onset. Therefore, the trend of offering solutions to the masses is slowly shifting towards tailor-made therapies, taking into consideration the genetic makeup of an individual. Over the past decade, personalized medicine has made significant inroads through tailoring disease management, treatment, and even prevention, by examining the genetic and environmental risks of a patient. Several factors are driving growth in the personalized medicine market, such as the advancements in genomics, metabolomics, and proteomics; development of targeted diagnostics and therapeutics; and completion of the human genome map. With advancements in technology, both software and hardware, personalized medicine has rapidly shifted from a potential clinical application into a reality. The ability to track, detect, and treat life threatening diseases in its early stages and improving the quality of life and survival rate of patients bodes well for the personalized medicine market. However, restraining factors do exist for the personalized medicine space that could potentially affect its future growth. These include primarily the regulatory norms and the high costs. Scientists are actively focusing on developing cost-effective genotyping methods that would guide physicians in customizing treatment. In addition, other major factors hindering the global personalized medicine market include highly expensive personalized medicine diagnostics for specific treatment. Moreover, a majority of patients, especially in the developing countries, are unaware of all of the recent advancements in personalized medicines.

‘Personalized medicine’ is a novel approach in the healthcare industry that enables physicians to devise safe and effective individualized therapies, based on the patient’s genetic composition. The concept is driven largely by the imaging industry, and in particular, the new molecular technologies. Molecular Medicine is a key tool for personalized medicine as it can effectively evaluate a patient’s response to therapy. Personalized medicine involves the testing of a patient’s tumors for chemical signatures, and expression of the tumors’ affinity to a specific class of peptides (for ex: somatostatin analogues) by SPECT or PET/CT. Once the results are obtained, a physician then devises individualized therapies based on a combination of peptides and therapeutic isotopes. Developed by ITG Isotope Technologies Garching GmbH, the Neuroendocrine Tumors Peptide Receptor Radio-Isotope Therapy (NET-PRRT) is one of the most successful examples of a radio-isotope based personalized therapy.

Presently, research and development in the therapeutic field has enabled radioisotopes to be utilized in immunotherapy drugs for cancer treatment.  In the coming years, growing interest in therapeutic radiopharmaceuticals is expected to lend traction to the development of highly focused tumor targeting techniques, and therapeutic isotopes uniquely tailored to optimize treatment of various tumors. There is also significant anticipation on the development of novel radiopharmaceuticals, intended to target specific biomarkers in order to ensure the early diagnosis and prognosis, enabling tailored therapies for each oncological patient.

Relevant Aspects of Radiopharmaceutical Production

At present, there are more than 100 radiopharmaceuticals which are developed using reactor or cyclotron derived radioisotopes and utilized for the diagnosis of common diseases and the treatment of specific diseases such as cancer. Radiopharmaceuticals preparation represents the handling of huge quantities of radioactive materials as well as chemical processing. Various aspects that are required to be considered in the manufacture of radiopharmaceuticals include handling of radioisotope preparation such as import, operation as well as maintenance of processing plants, to comply with cGMP (current good manufacturing practices) codes, guaranteeing efficient quality assurance/quality control (QA/QC) systems, product registration with national as well as regional health authorities as well as radioactive material transport.

Unlike traditional production of pharmaceuticals, production of radiopharmaceuticals continues to be on a comparatively smaller scale. Adoption of cGMP procedures used in drugs industry is challenging and expensive as it requires deployment of skilled personnel, controlled materials as well as procedures, accessibility to certified equipment, manufacture of the products in selected clean spaces, use of validated procedures and analytical techniques, complete certification of the process and launch of the end product by a qualified professional.

The task of addressing the requirements for clean room in radioisotope laboratories particularly hot cells is technically challenging, to match with the requirements for radiological as well as pharmaceutical security. During the past decade, the market witnessed growth in the usage of PET in the routine diagnostic imaging, and the corresponding usage of PET radiopharmaceuticals, especially fluorine-18 fluorodeoxy glucose (or 18F-FDG). Related 511 keV ultra-energy radiation requires heavier shielding and highly advanced handling devices. Due to short ‘half-life’, the focus is on process validation and stringent adherence to appropriate procedures in handling all stages instead of depending only on the ultimate QC test results. The requirement for faster, remote and consistent PET radiopharmaceuticals synthesis has resulted in the launch of microprocessor-enabled automated synthesis components.

Tracer production is gradually shifting towards automated synthesis modules with disposable cassette/kit system. The approach is beneficial in terms of automation, regulatory compliance, multi-Curie handling, and improved radiation safety. Nonetheless, the need to reconfigure the modules for production of new radiotracers at research level poses a major challenge for radiochemists. In 2017, Optimized Radiochemical Applications (ORA), a Belgian company introduced new NEPTIS® xSeed™ kitless synthesizer designed for development of new tracers at economical cost by eliminating the need for cassettes/kits at the research stage.
Regulatory Regime Governing Production and Use of Radiopharmaceuticals

Radiopharmaceuticals are governed by a regulatory regime that is characterized with high degree of stringency, lack of harmony and regional variances in terms of product type, intended use and risk factors. The framework requires companies to engage with regulators during the early stages of the development cycle. Radiopharmaceuticals in the US are supervised by various agencies like the FDA and the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC). While the former authorizes the application of radiopharmaceuticals and diagnostics for investigational trials, the latter licenses the use and possession of radioactive materials in the country. Sponsors planning clinical studies for investigational radiopharmaceuticals are required to secure permission from Institutional Review Board and file for Investigational New Drug (IND) application. On the other hand, jurisdiction over specific products is controlled by specific offices or divisions of the FDA. However, radiopharmaceuticals are considered as standard pharmaceutical products while seeking marketing approval. Sponsors generally seek approval through Biologics License Applications (BLAs) and New Drug Applications (NDAs). Moreover, the 505(b) (1) pathway focuses on efficacy and safety. The US FDA and European Medicines Agency (EMA) agree on toxicology studies on a single relevant species. These regulatory agencies also acknowledge potential of radiopharmaceuticals to damage DNA. On the contrary, the FDA and EMA holds difference related to Good Laboratory Practice (GLP) guidelines, with the former agency remaining flexible in admitting non-GLP toxicology studies.

Proper precautions are need to be taken during production, storage, and application of radioisotopes as these compounds generate radiation, the exposure to which can harm people and the environment. Due to their harmful effects, radioisotopes are strictly governed by regulations in several parts of the world. Approval from authorities such as the Food and Drug Administration (United States), the Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency (United Kingdom), and Therapeutic Goods Administration (Australia) are compulsory for any production, usage, or storage of radioisotopes. The Nuclear Regulatory Commission, an independent agency of the U.S. government, cites that about 6,000 licenses are registered for usage of radioactive materials, and any violation of agreement can lead to enforcement actions including revoking of license.

In the EU, Clinical Trial Regulation EU No. 536/2014, brought forward in 2014, has made certain amendments to the radiopharmaceuticals sector. The new regulation has invalidated the need to have authorization to manufacture and import investigational medicinal products and made it much easier for preparing diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals when applied as Investigation Medicinal Products (IMPs) in clinical trials. The regulation also invalidated the need for GMP production of the radiopharmaceuticals, simplified the labelling of diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals used as Investigation Medicinal Products (IMPs) or auxiliary medicinal products (AMPs).

In Q1 2020, Therapeutic Goods Administration announced relaxation of the rules pertaining to radiopharmaceutical production in response to the breakdown in interstate commerce during the COVID-19 pandemic in Australia. Previously, hospitals can quickly source radiopharmaceuticals from vendors located in other states of the country. However, due to the curbs imposed to contain the spread of COVID-19 pandemic, air traffic was affected, making it difficult for hospitals to access radiopharmaceuticals, which usually entail short shelf life of about six hours. TGA’s revised regulations exempted radiopharmaceuticals from some manufacturing requirements, allowing the manufacture of these compounds by certain pharmacists, medical practitioners, and radiochemists for use within Australia.
