DIVERSITY, EQUITY & INCLUSION (DEI)

It’s Time To Fix The DEI Dilemma & Create Strong Businesses With Healthy Workplaces

We live in an increasingly complex and interconnected world where shaped by globalization and technological advancements diversity has become the fabric of modern society. Companies eager to establish a strong presence in a variety of emerging new markets have initiated the global gold rush for multicultural markets, diverse employees, and untapped consumer bases. Success in global markets requires multicultural initiatives and a sustained effort to integrate distinct demographic and cultural diversity into an inclusive environment that fits into the broader vision of the company’s growth and advancement. This is opening up rich opportunities for companies to spearhead growth by leveraging their access to a diverse talent pool. While global diversity of the labor force is good and beneficial for companies to enhance creativity and drive innovation, the same also challenges long held traditional business ideas and assumptions. Companies are being forced to effect major structural reorganizations spanning all key areas of business operations, marketing, talent recruitment and retention functions.

Interestingly, despite the growing levels of diversity and interconnectedness, there is also growing polarization fueled by identity politics and resurgence of nationalist ideals. Workplaces tend to mirror the sociocultural dynamics at play in our lives outside work. At a time like the present, the importance, value and positive impact of DEI in modern enterprises can no longer be debated or undermined. Even amid rapid globalization and changing perceptions about diversity, ethnic minorities continue to face discrimination in the labor market and as the global fight towards a fair and just society heats up, companies are feeling the urgency to renew their sense of responsibility to offer DEI in the workplace. Major social and political changes as evidenced by the “Black Lives Matter” protest rallies which erupted around the world, means companies need to reevaluate how diverse and inclusive their workforce really is. Employers now have a responsibility to protect employees from harassment and discrimination in the workplace based on race, gender or color. Diversity in the business environment today goes beyond just gender, race and ethnicity. It includes employees with diverse religious and political beliefs, education, socioeconomic backgrounds, sexual orientation, cultures and even disabilities.

Even from a business standpoint, the case for diversity in workplace is overwhelming given its ability to foster innovation, creativity and empathy in ways that homogeneous environments can never do. As markets evolve to become more competitive than ever before and the business environment gets tougher amid the crisis, companies need to remain alert, aware, agile and responsive to remain afloat, profitable and successful. To be successful in a world that is constantly changing, companies need to be leading the change rather than responding to it. Companies with more diverse teams and inclusive are better able to lead change than those with a more homogeneous workforce. A strong organizational culture and robust structural foundation are critical for achieving agility that comes from internal strength. Diverse companies earn 2.5 times higher cash flow per employee and inclusive teams are productive by over 35%. High-diversity environments and all-inclusive culture has clear benefits and advantages.

DEI Equals Better Decision Making:- DEI is at the heart of better business decisions. The more diverse people are and the more included they are in the system, the more decisions get challenged. Infinite dissimilarities distinguish individuals and when viewed with multiple lenses better decisions evolve. In comparison, homogenous groups are prone to group thinking which can compromise the leverage that comes from greater variety of perspectives and increases the odds of information not being more thoroughly and accurately processed. Companies with diverse and inclusive teams enjoy better business decision outcomes almost 90% of the time, given that the risk of decisions getting sidetracked by biases is significantly lower. It is therefore of little surprise that team diversity is becoming the architectural principle guiding decision-making processes. When employees feel included in the workplace, they experience a sense of being valued, trusted, and safe in hold differing views and making mistakes without being penalized. This increases involvement in decision making, and willingness to take risks and address tough issues or challenges.

DEI Fosters Innovation:- DEI strategies can help companies sharpen their competitive edge asdiverse teams bring in higher productivity and even higher quality of results. DEI also drive innovation and performance. This new radical concept of equality, greater self-awareness, emotional intelligence, ability to shift one’s personal vision, mindsets & behavior and treat differences as strength, goes beyond helping solve organizational problems to achieve slated goals. DEI brings life to organizational values, mission and purpose, and assists companies in helping clients not just meet their goals but helping them be future ready.

DEI Increases Workplace Happiness:- Paychecks and traditional employment benefits can no longer keep the new generation of young, ambitious, and forward-thinking employees happy and motivated. Modern workforce comprising of new millennials today prefer a work environment where their voices are heard and their unique differences valued and appreciated. The importance of DEI for an employee can be put into perspective by the fact that the decision to work for a company is strongly guided by the prospective company’s commitment to DEI. Several reasons underlie the fact why DEI is the recipe for a happy workforce. A inclusive workplace promotes and prioritizes employee well-being. An empowered employee who feels as a welcomed part of the team is motivated to perform the best every day. Diverse cross-cultural teams bring creativity and innovation, which results into successful project outcomes and increased revenue for companies. Teams led by ethnically diverse leaders are over 45% more likely to outperform competitors. When companies improve the working environment and create an inclusive workplace, people not only feel empowered, but they also band together and work harder to meet the company’s target goals. A stable and happy workforce means not just more productivity but also lesser attrition for the company. Happy employees help businesses avoid employee turnover costs. Employees part of diverse and inclusive teams are 30% more likely to remain loyal to the company.

DEI Leads to Better Profits: Diverse management boards and executive teams bring about 40% higher EBIT and are 35% more likely to create long-term value for shareholders and investors. Gender-diverse executive teams are especially capable of outperforming male-dominated companies by over 25 times. Cash flows of diverse and inclusive companies are 3.2 times higher than companies with more traditional homogenous staff. Investing in people as the most valuable asset will help entire industries to achieve long-term growth beyond the immediate advantages of employee retention and increased productivity. Embedding inclusive values into corporate culture can help entire industries to reach new heights. A positive top-down change can send ripples of change for the better across an industry and business world in general.

Yet, global DEI initiatives are particularly challenging to successfully implement and measure. Achieving real equality and inclusion is a challenging task and over 65% of employees are of the opinion that their managers do not foster an inclusive environment. Achieving greater diversity in the workplace is relatively easier as it represents the mix or the make-up of an entity. However, the inclusion piece of the puzzle is more complex and ensuring the same is immensely tough as it involves integration of the contributions, presence and perspectives of different groups of people into the environment. Inclusion is all about making the mix work together as a cohesive whole by respecting, welcoming and valuing the diversity of every individual’s unique background, experiences, race, ethnicity, sexual orientation, gender identity, faith, tradition, and most importantly perspectives. An inclusive culture allows for collaborative daily learnings, full freedom to decide and experiment, provides excellent mobility across the organization, and encourages long term thinking. Diversity without inclusion can create a toxic culture and similarly inclusion without diversity can create an uncreative and infertile environment where a company can stagnant. Prevalence of microaggression is one of the main indications of a culture lacking inclusion. The importance of understanding microaggression is therefore critical. Microaggression can take on several forms such as everyday verbal and nonverbal slights, insults or snubs deliberately aimed at hurting people. Macroaggressions are indicative of hostility towards certain marginalized groups of individuals and kills the sense of belonging in teams or in the office or workplace. In what is a major setback to most DEI programs, a large percentage of companies have no mechanisms or systems in place to identify and address microaggressions. Some of the other key reasons why corporate DEI initiatives fail include reactive attitude to DEI; implementation of DEI as mandatory policy to ensure compliance; one size fits all program strategy; poorly funded programs and lack of commitment from top tier management; a "culture fit" attitude that leads to prejudiced exclusion of diverse talent; lack of tools to monitor and measure the effectiveness of DEI programs; inability to identify barriers to DEI and unwillingness to promptly act to overcome the same; lack of mentoring mechanisms to address bias both implicit and unconscious biases; and integration of DEI programs as part of HR and corporate compliance agendas. Trying to close a diversity gap with just a performance metric will not result in sustainable and effective long-term DEI.

Who Should Lead the Push for Progress on DEI?
Building an inclusive team culture is key and is emerging to be a major business priority with companies seeking to successfully implement DEI programs. Until now DEI as an initiative was owned exclusively by the HR department wherein recruitment policies favored diverse candidates to get the widest possible talent pool and expertise. HR driven DEI programs are however short-termed with zero ability to bring in long-lasting results. Post recruitment, it’s the role of a manager which influences inclusion. Inclusive leadership is only way to foster DEI on a continuous basis in the workplace. From merely issuing directives to creating and empowering internal change agents, a revolutionary change in leadership is underway. Key traits of an inclusive manager in a diverse workforce include humility, awareness and cognizance of one’s own unconscious biases; cultural awareness and intelligence; ability to self-diagnose problems, develop system awareness, identify organizational blindspots and design appropriate interventions; building trust and empowering team members; and encouraging collaboration.

For Driving Successful DEI Programs, Here’s What Every Company Should Do?
Devoting adequate corporate time and resources to DEI is an absolute necessity for companies. Diversity programs must include the four As’- Analysis, Action Planning, Alignment, and Assessment. Companies must begin with analyzing their internal structures and identify DEI gaps. Information can be gathered through focus group sessions or surveys to learn what is missing and what practices can be put into place. An action plan comprising strategic goals should be built based on the gap analysis performed, clearly outlining strategies to reach the defined goals. The strategic action plan needs to be aligned with the existing goals, objectives, infrastructure and value system of the company. All divisions and departments at all levels must have access to the DEI initiatives and should be integrated into everyday part of operations. Implementation results must be continuously assessed and measured and simultaneously accountability tools need to be developed. Measuring progressing toward the goal, identifying and overcoming barriers and challenges that hinder progress are of utmost importance and without it DEI can integrate into a mere passing trend. To successfully create an inclusive environment, a top-down approach is crucial beginning with the management board and senior leadership. Senior leaders in the company should be engaged and trained to perform inclusive leadership roles and should be held accountable. Small acts of inclusiveness should be recognized, appreciated and celebrated and also incentivized. Equally important as modelling inclusive leadership is being recognized for it. The goal of every company should be to build sustainable DEI initiatives. A DEI initiative becomes sustainable only when it is supported by the entire organization. Finally, to make real and meaningful progress, business leaders must begin to believe that businesses have the responsibility to look beyond just profits and make a positive impact on people and society.

DEI IN THE WORKPLACE: A FACT CHECK

  • Over 65% of employees would like to see their company increase DEI efforts.
  • Close to 7% of all Fortune 500 companies have women as their CEOs.
  • A diverse & inclusive management board helps boosts company revenues by over 20%.
  • Companies with healthy racial and ethnic diversity are over 70 times likely to successfully capture new markets & secure new revenue streams.
  • A company with an equal gender mix and treatment can generate 50% additional revenue.
  • An inclusive company has a 130% chance of achieving its financial target.
  • Diverse & inclusive teams make better decisions 90% of the time. An all-male group of decision makers make right decisions only 47% of the time, while gender diversity in the team increases the effectiveness index to 70%. Age plus gender diversity increases the effectiveness index further to 80% and age plus gender plus demographic diversity increases the effectiveness and quality of decisions to 90%.
  • 35% of women in leadership positions or in the “C-suite” can bring additional 8% increase in net profit margin for a company.
  • Over 80% of companies believe that DEI can offer a competitive advantage in the marketplace. Yet approximately 92% of CEOs are white men in the United States & men are 35% more likely to be promoted to a managerial role, indicating gender discrimination in the workplace and underlining the fact that DEI still remains hugely neglected.
  • In the United States, 75% of baby boomers in the workforce are white. This in contrast to new millennials in workforce where only 57% are white, indicating a gradual but sure shift to diversity.
  • By the year 2025, 78% of the workforce will be made up of the millennial generation.
  • Over 65% of employees in the USA have experienced workplace discrimination while in UK and France, the figures stand at 55% and 46% respectively.
  • The most common triggers for discrimination at the workplace are gender, age, racism and sexual orientation each accounting for 43%, 48%, 36% and 25% of the times.
